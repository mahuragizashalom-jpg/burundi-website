Lake Tanganyika

The ancient freshwater giant and biodiversity hotspot of the Great Lakes.

← Back to Overview

Shared by Burundi, the Democratic Republic of the Congo, Tanzania, and Zambia, Lake Tanganyika is a true wonder of the natural world. It is the world's longest freshwater lake (673 km) and the second deepest (1,470 m), containing an incredible 17% of the world's available surface freshwater.

An Evolutionary Lab: Cichlid Fish

Lake Tanganyika is famous among scientists and aquarium enthusiasts for its extraordinary biodiversity. It is home to over 250 species of cichlid fish, remarkable because 98% of them are endemic—meaning they are found nowhere else on Earth. These fish have evolved into a stunning variety of shapes, colors, and behaviors, making the lake a living laboratory of evolution similar to the Galapagos Islands.

Biodiversity and Beyond

Beneath its crystal-clear surface, the lake supports over 1,500 species of flora and fauna. This includes dozens of non-cichlid fish species, freshwater crabs, shrimp, and even jellyfish. The lake's shores are equally vibrant, providing habitat for hippos, crocodiles, and thousands of migratory birds that flock to the sandy beaches of Burundi's coastline.

Gustave: The Living Legend

One of the lake's most notorious residents is Gustave, a giant Nile crocodile rumored to be over 20 feet long. While his exploits have become the stuff of legend and even a documentary, he serves as a powerful symbol of the untamed and ancient nature of the lake's ecosystem.

A Vital Lifeblood

For the people of Burundi, Lake Tanganyika is much more than a scenic wonder; it is a vital lifeblood. It provides a primary source of protein through its industrial and artisanal fisheries, yields water for the capital city, and serves as an important transport route connecting Burundi to its neighbors. The preservation of this ancient giant is critical for the future of the entire region.
