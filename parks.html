National Parks

From misty montane forests to vast savannahs, exploring the wild heart of Burundi.

← Back to Overview

Burundi's natural landscape is a diverse mosaic of ecosystems. To preserve this heritage, the nation has established several national parks that protect rare wildlife and critical water sources for the entire region.

Kibira: The Emerald Crown

Located in the northwest, Kibira National Park is a high-altitude montane rainforest that sits atop the Congo-Nile Divide. Covering 400 square kilometers, it is the continuation of Rwanda's Nyungwe Forest. Kibira is a misty, emerald world home to over 640 plant species and 98 species of mammals.

The park's most famous residents are its wild chimpanzees. While their population has faced challenges, they remain a symbol of the park's wild core. Visitors can also spot black-and-white colobus monkeys, blue monkeys, and over 200 species of birds, making it a paradise for birdwatchers.

Ruvubu: The Savannah Giant

In contrast to the dense forests of Kibira is Ruvubu National Park, Burundi's largest protected area. Stretching across 500 square kilometers in the northeast, the park is named after the Ruvubu River that winds through it. Here, the landscape opens up into vast savannahs, riverine forests, and wetlands.

Ruvubu is home to large mammals that are found nowhere else in Burundi today. Lucky visitors can see hippopotamuses wallowing in the river, herds of cape buffalo grazing in the grasslands, and various species of antelope like waterbucks and bushbucks. Over 400 species of birds have been recorded here, including the rare Shoebill stork.

Conservation Challenges and Hope

Both parks are vital not just for wildlife, but for the Burundian people. Kibira regulates the climate and provides water for the country's hydropower plants and tea plantations. While poaching and habitat loss remain threats, ongoing conservation efforts and the growing interest in eco-tourism are providing new hope for the preservation of these emerald treasures.
