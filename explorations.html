Historic Explorations

The legendary meeting of explorers that captivated the world.

← Back to Overview

During the 19th century, Central Africa was the final frontier for European explorers. Burundi, with its strategic location along Lake Tanganyika and its proximity to the rumored sources of the Nile, became a focal point of these legendary expeditions.

The Quest for the Nile

The source of the Nile was one of the greatest geographical mysteries of the Victorian era. Explorers like David Livingstone spent years trekking through the continent, enduring disease, hostile environments, and isolation to find the headwaters of the world's longest river.

Livingstone and Stanley in Burundi

In November 1871, shortly after their famous first meeting in Ujiji (Tanzania), Dr. David Livingstone and the journalist Henry Morton Stanley traveled together to what is now Burundi. They spent several days exploring the northern shores of Lake Tanganyika, hoping to find a river that flowed out of the lake and northward toward the Nile.

The Mugere Meeting Point

Located just 12 kilometers south of Bujumbura is the Livingstone-Stanley Monument at Mugere. This site marks where the two explorers spent two nights from November 25-27, 1871. A large rock carved with the date commemorates their stay. The explorers described the hospitality of the local Burundians as some of the most welcoming they had experienced in all their travels.

Exploratory Legacy

While we now know that Lake Tanganyika is not the source of the Nile, the visit by Livingstone and Stanley placed Burundi on the map of world exploration. Their detailed journals and sketches provided the first written descriptions of the region's breathtaking landscapes and sophisticated society to a global audience.
